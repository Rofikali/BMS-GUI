Lesson 5 — Accounts

Until now we've been talking about Assets, Liabilities, Revenue, etc.

Now let's answer a question.

What exactly is an account?

Imagine Your Shop

Suppose your business has these items:

Cash
Rice
Sugar
Dal
Potato
Computer
Printer
Bank Loan
Sales
Rent
Electricity
Internet

If I ask:

"How much cash do you have?"

Where will you look?

In the Cash Account.

If I ask:

"How much rice is left?"

You'll look in the Inventory Account.

If I ask:

"How much do you owe the bank?"

You'll look in the Bank Loan Account.

👉 Every important thing in a business gets its own account.

Think of an account as a dedicated record that tracks one thing over time.

As a Software Engineer

Think about Python.

If I write:

cash = 50000
inventory = 25000
loan = 10000
sales = 0

Each variable stores one type of information.

Accounting does exactly the same thing.

Instead of variables, it uses accounts.

Why Separate Accounts?

Imagine at the end of the month:

Business Revenue
Grocery ₹80,000
Computer ₹45,000
Printing ₹12,000
Freelancing ₹90,000

Now you know:

👉 Freelancing made the most revenue.

Without separate accounts, you'd only know:

Total Sales = ₹227,000

That number is much less useful for decision-making.

Real CEO Thinking

Let's imagine you're the CEO of your own company.

You ask your accountant:

"Why was profit lower this month?"

The accountant doesn't say:

"I don't know."

Instead, they open the accounts and say:

Electricity expense increased by ₹3,000.
Computer sales dropped by ₹15,000.
Grocery sales stayed the same.
Printing income increased.

Now you can make decisions based on facts.

That's why accounting exists.
